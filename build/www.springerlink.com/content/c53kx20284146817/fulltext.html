Original article

Clinical impact of endoscopic papillectomy for benign–malignant borderline lesions of the major duodenal papilla

Megumi Harano1, Shomei Ryozawa1 , Hirotoshi Iwano1, Kumiko Taba1, Manabu Sen-yo1 and Isao Sakaida1

(1)  Department of Gastroenterology and Hepatology, Yamaguchi University Graduate School of Medicine, 1-1-1 Minamikogushi, Ube Yamaguchi, 755-8505, Japan

Shomei Ryozawa
Email: ryozawa@yamaguchi-u.ac.jp

Published online: 18 September 2010

Abstract
Background and purpose  
The present study retrospectively analyzed the clinical impact of endoscopic papillectomy on the selection of a treatment strategy for patients with benign–malignant borderline lesions of the major duodenal papilla.
Patients and methods  
Between November 1995 and July 2009, 28 patients were selected for endoscopic papillectomy. The clinical impact of endoscopic papillectomy was assessed. Snare resection was performed in a radical fashion.
Results  
An endoscopic papillectomy was technically feasible in all patients. En bloc excision was achieved in 22 cases (79%). The final histopathological diagnoses of the endoscopic specimen were 17 adenoma (61%), 7 carcinoma in adenoma (25%), and 4 adenocarcinoma (14%). Two out of the four adenocarcinoma cases were referred for surgery. The other two patients with negative margins have not experienced recurrences during the follow-up period. A residual tumor was detected in 1 out of 17 cases (6%) of adenoma and 2 out of 7 cases (29%) of carcinoma in adenoma.
Conclusions  
Endoscopic papillectomy is therefore considered to be an effective treatment for patients with a benign–malignant borderline lesion of the major duodenal papilla. This method also has an important clinical impact because it provides an accurate diagnosis, aids in the selection of an appropriate treatment strategy, and reduces unnecessary surgery.

Keywords  Endoscopic papillectomy – Major duodenal papilla – Tumor of the major duodenal papilla

Introduction

Tumors of the main duodenal papilla are rare, with a prevalence of only 0.04–0.12% in autopsy studies [1]. These tumors represent 5% of all gastrointestinal tumors [2], but are being identified at a higher frequency with the increasing use of upper endoscopic examination and endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP). Of the wide variety of benign papillary tumors, adenoma is the most common. Because of its premalignant potential [3, 4], complete removal is essential for curative therapy.

Endoscopic papillectomy has been recognized to be a safe and reliable treatment modality for benign tumors of the main duodenal papilla [5–18]. The advantage of this method is that it is less invasive, thus removing the need for general anesthesia and laparotomy. It is commonly agreed that the indication for an endoscopic papillectomy is adenoma of the papilla of Vater, which is confined to the ampullary region. However, it is difficult to establish a diagnosis of adenoma of the papilla of Vater. The association of the carcinoma is well-recognized and is detected in 25–43% of adenomas of the papilla of Vater [15, 19]. Several studies have addressed the problem of the low diagnostic accuracy of endoscopic forceps biopsies in the differentiation between benign adenomas and carcinomas [4, 19, 20]. In that sense, an endoscopic forceps biopsy cannot provide sufficient information in relation to the resectability of the tumor by endoscopic papillectomy.

Another view holds that endoscopic papillectomy can be indicated for the purpose of total biopsy [21]. In 2006, a guideline on the role of endoscopy in ampullary and duodenal adenomas was published by the Standards of Practice Committee of the American Society for Gastrointestinal Endoscopy [16]. However, it does not mention the diagnostic role of endoscopic papillectomy as total biopsy. Moreover, it does not clarify how many patients could avoid additional surgery after an endoscopic papillectomy.

Previous reports considered endoscopic papillectomy to be a treatment modality mainly for benign adenomas, and they lack insight into its clinical impact as a total biopsy. The present study retrospectively analyzed the clinical impact of endoscopic papillectomy on the selection of a treatment strategy for patients with benign–malignant borderline lesions of the major duodenal papilla.

Patients and methods
Patients

Between November 1995 and July 2009, 28 patients with a benign–malignant borderline lesion of the major duodenal papilla who met the following criteria were selected for endoscopic papillectomy: (1) benign–malignant borderline histologic findings on the forceps biopsy (minimum of five biopsies), with a biopsy diagnosis of benign–malignant borderline corresponding to category 3 (noninvasive low grade neoplasia; low grade adenoma/dysplasia) or category 4.1 (noninvasive high grade neoplasia; high grade adenoma/dysplasia) according to the Vienna classification of gastrointestinal epithelial neoplasia [22]; (2) tumor size less than 4 cm; (3) no endoscopic evidence of malignancy (e.g., ulceration, excessive friability, and spontaneous bleeding); (4) the absence of extension into the pancreatic or biliary ducts and extension beyond the duodenal muscularis propria on the evaluation using endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) and endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) and/or intraductal ultrasound (IDUS).

The patients included 17 male patients and 11 female patients, with a median age of 66.6 years of age (range 51–79 years of age). All patients gave their informed consent to the procedure and were aware of the option of surgical treatment. The current retrospective study was approved by our institutional review board.

Methods

Prior to performing endoscopic papillectomy, the platelet count, prothrombin time, and partial thromboplastin time were measured to confirm the absence of a tendency toward bleeding. Endoscopic papillectomy was performed by three endoscopists (S. R., H. I., M. H.) who each had at least 5 years of experience in interventional endoscopy. Patients were placed in the prone position, intravenously sedated with midazolam and/or propofol, and monitored by pulsoxymetry.

An endoscopic papillectomy was performed with the Olympus side-viewing endoscopes with a working channel of 3.7 or 4.2 mm (TJF-200, JF-260V, or TJF-260V, Olympus Medical Systems, Tokyo, Japan), and a standard monofilament polypectomy snare (SD-5L-1, Olympus Medical Systems, Tokyo, Japan) was used with the microprocessor-controlled electrosurgical generator (ICC 200, ERBE, Tübingen, Germany). The output was set for Endocut mode, effect 3 (output limit 120 W), and soft coagulation (output limit 30 W). Submucosal injection of physiologic saline solution into the submucosal layer of the surrounding duodenum of the duodenal papilla was performed in some cases to reduce the risk of duodenal perforation. Snare resection was performed in a radical fashion. Lesions that could not be resected en bloc were resected in a piecemeal fashion. The resected specimen was submitted for histological analysis by serial sectioning. A 7-Fr biliary stent and a 5-Fr pancreatic stent were placed in the common bile duct and in the pancreatic duct in all patients with accessible ducts.

Results
Technical results
An endoscopic papillectomy was technically feasible in all patients. Table 1 shows the technical results of the endoscopic papillectomy. The median size of the tumor was 1.7 cm (range 0.4–4 cm). Submucosal injection was performed in 12 (43%) out of the 28 cases. En bloc excision was achieved in 22 cases (79%). Biliary stents were placed in 21 patients (75%), and pancreatic stents were placed in 23 patients (82%).

Complications
Nine procedure-related early complications after an endoscopic papillectomy occurred: hemorrhage in 5 cases (18%), acute pancreatitis in 2 cases (7%), and acute cholangitis in 2 cases (7%) out of 28 cases. Perforation did not occur in any patients (Table 5). Of the five subjects who experienced hemorrhage, four patients required emergency endoscopic hemostasis using hemoclips and/or the local injection of epinephrine, while one patient had only clinically evident bleeding and did not require endoscopic hemostasis. Of the two patients who experienced pancreatitis, one subject had mild pancreatitis and another had severe pancreatitis. Both were patients in whom we could not place a pancreatic stent after endoscopic papillectomy. Both patients who experienced cholangitis had not received a biliary stent after the endoscopic papillectomy.

Discussion

In this series, we revealed the results of endoscopic papillectomies that we performed on 28 cases of benign–malignant borderline lesion of the major duodenal papilla. All 28 cases were resectable, and an en bloc excision was successfully performed in 79% of the cases. The reported success rates range from 33 to 100% [5–12, 15, 17, 18]. It can be said that careful patient selection, based on the macroscopic findings, size, and location of tumors, is critical for successful papillectomy [7, 9, 11].

A technical review reported complications related to endoscopic papillectomy, including hemorrhage, pancreatitis, cholangitis, and perforation, to occur in 23.0% of patients (range 10–58%) [15]. Most bleeding can be controlled by conservative management and endoscopic hemostasis as in our cases except one who required transfusion [6]. Endoscopic papillectomy is associated with an increased risk for postprocedural pancreatitis. In our study, pancreatitis occurred in two patients in whom we could not place pancreatic duct stents after the papillectomy. The results of many studies suggest that the placement of a pancreatic duct stent reduces the risk of pancreatitis after endoscopic papillectomy [6, 7, 9, 11, 12, 15]. On the other hand, others advocate pancreatic duct stent placement only if delayed drainage of the pancreatic duct is noted after endoscopic papillectomy [5, 8]. The only prospective, randomized, controlled trial to evaluate the role of prophylactic pancreatic duct stent placement after endoscopic papillectomy showed a statistically significant decrease in the rate of postprocedure pancreatitis in the stent group [23]. On the basis of these data, prophylactic pancreatic duct stent placement is recommended to reduce the risk of postprocedural pancreatitis. Similar to the prevention of post-papillectomy pancreatitis, the placement of a biliary stent after endoscopic papillectomy may prevent post-papillectomy cholangitis. We placed a prophylactic biliary stent in 75% of the patients, and the two patients who experienced cholangitis in our series had not received a biliary stent after undergoing endoscopic papillectomy. Although little evidence is yet available, prophylactic biliary stent placement should nevertheless be considered if the bile duct orifice is not clearly visible and there is difficulty in cannulation after performing a resection of the tumor [15]. In our series, the submucosal injection of saline was performed in 12 (43%) out of the 28 cases and we experienced no instance of perforation. It may be useful for the safe resection of flat lesions surrounding the papilla [13, 15]. However, Hirooka et al. [12] described that local injection into the submucosa makes the procedure rather difficult to perform.

According to a report by Yamaguchi et al., final diagnoses in cases of benign–malignant borderline by biopsy diagnosis were as follows: adenoma in 14%, carcinoma in adenoma in 60%, and adenocarcinoma in 26%—the proportion of carcinoma was higher than that of adenoma [16]. In contrast, in our cases, the proportion of adenoma was higher, which was due to the fact that patients with no endoscopic findings indicating malignant tumors such as ulceration, excessive friability, and spontaneous bleeding, were selected. Therefore, it is believed that the indication criteria were appropriately established from the perspective of excluding carcinoma as much as possible.

Based on our experience, it is believed that, in cases for which the final diagnosis following an endoscopic papillectomy is adenocarcinoma, the surgical margin is positive, or the depth of invasion has extended beyond Oddi’s sphincter, then additional surgery following an endoscopic papillectomy is recommended. In addition, in cases for which the final diagnosis after endoscopic papillectomy is adenocarcinoma, the depth of invasion has been localized within the mucosa, an endoscopic papillectomy was performed for en bloc resection, and the resection stump is negative, it is highly possible that additional surgery is not required.

A complete endoscopic resection of the tumor of the major duodenal papilla is defined as the absence of an endoscopically visible and histologically proven residual tumor for a follow-up of 3–6 months [11, 15]. In our series, we treated the lesions that were detected during a 1–2-month follow-up as “residual tumors” rather than “recurrence.” Whether en bloc or piecemeal resection is the best method for successful endoscopic papillectomy remains unresolved. It also is not clear whether the recurrence rates differ according to the method of endoscopic resection. Nevertheless, en bloc resection is fundamental to the treatment of neoplastic lesions because it enables the complete removal of the lesion with the advantage of submitting complete tissue for more precise histopathologic examination [15]. Moreover en bloc resection has the advantage of potentially shortening the procedure time, thus requiring less electrocautery, and resulting in fewer complications [24]. In our series, a residual tumor was observed in one case of adenoma in which an en bloc resection had been performed. In this case, the slight development of a lesion that could not be visually confirmed may have existed in the pancreatic duct or in the direction of the bile duct. Therefore, first surveillance endoscopy is recommended at shorter intervals, e.g., at 1 and 3 month intervals even after an en bloc resection [7, 15]. Follow-up endoscopy with biopsy should be scheduled at 3–6 month intervals for 1 year after endoscopic papillectomy and then at yearly intervals thereafter in the absence of recurrence [5, 6, 11, 13, 15].

Overall, of the 28 cases in which we performed an endoscopic papillectomy, 23 cases (82%) were curative, with the exception of 2 cases of additional surgery and 3 cases of residual tumors. The 3 cases of residual tumors were cured with endoscopic therapy and, as a result, 26 cases (93%) were successfully treated with endoscopic therapy and therefore required no additional surgery.

In cases of benign–malignant borderline lesions of the major duodenal papilla, it would be reasonable to perform an endoscopic papillectomy, which has a low procedure-related risk. With an endoscopic papillectomy, an adequate histologic evaluation of the entire lesion can be conducted. Once complete removal and absence of malignancy have been confirmed based on histologic examinations, then an endoscopic papillectomy should be curative. On the other hand, it is believed that, when it is judged not to be curative, then additional endoscopic therapy or surgery should be considered.

Endoscopic papillectomy is an effective treatment for patients with a benign–malignant borderline lesion of the major duodenal papilla. This method also plays an important clinical role by providing an accurate diagnosis, thereby helping in the subsequent selection of an appropriate treatment strategy and reducing any unnecessary surgery.
