Is Endoscopic Papillectomy Safe for Ampullary Adenomas with High-Grade Dysplasia?

Ji Hun Kim1, Jin Hong Kim2, Jae Ho Han3, Byung Moo Yoo2, Myung Wook Kim1 and Wook Hwan Kim1 

(1)  Departments of Surgery, School of Medicine, Ajou University, Suwon, Korea
(2)  Departments of Gastroenterology, School of Medicine, Ajou University, Suwon, Korea
(3)  Departments of Pathology, School of Medicine, Ajou University, Suwon, Korea

Abstract
Objectives  This study was designed to provide safe management guidelines for ampullary adenoma by analysis of clinicopathological features.
Background  The treatment of ampullary cancer has been established; however, the indications for treatment of ampullary adenoma remain controversial.
Methods  Between July 1997 and July 2008, a total of 33 patients were diagnosed with ampullary adenoma prior to procedures: 20 endoscopic papillectomies (ESP), 5 transduodenal resections (TDR), and 8 pancreatoduodenectomies (PD).
Results  The false-negative rate of biopsy for cancer was 27.5% (8/29). Coexistence of cancer in patients with pre-high-grade dysplasia (HGD) was 50.0% (5/10), whereas it was 15.7% in pre-low-grade dysplasia (LGD). In addition, the rate of recurrence was 80% (8/10) in patients with pre-HGD. The size of tumor by final pathology was 1.27 ± 0.89 cm in LGD, 1.81 ± 0.99 cm in HGD, and 1.98 ± 1.08 cm in cancer group. There was a significant correlation between size of tumor and final pathology (P = 0.036). According to receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve, criterion to predict HGD/cancer was tumor size larger than 1.5 cm; sensitivity and specificity were 55.6% and 80.0%, respectively, and likelihood ratio was 2.778. However, size of tumor was not associated with preprocedural pathology.
Conclusions  Ampullary adenoma with preprocedural HGD was highly associated with coexistence of cancer and recurrence. Moreover, most of large tumors were treated by surgical procedures and proved to be cancer. Therefore, we suggest that ampullary adenoma with preprocedural HGD or more than 1.5 cm should not be managed with endoscopic papillectomy due to high associated rates of recurrence.

Since 1935, pancreatoduodenectomy (PD) has been used as the treatment of choice for periampullary tumors.1,2 For the treatment of ampullary cancer, Beger et al. reported that oncological resection of ampullary cancer by means of PD is the surgical procedure of choice; the 3- and 5-year survival rates were 72% and 52%, respectively.3 Moreover, Yoon et al. reported that PD should preferably be performed even in early ampullary cancer.4 The management criteria for ampullary cancer have already been established.

As a noninvasive treatment, transduodenal resection (TDR) is usually indicated in patients with high operative risk.3 However, most surgeons have no great desire to try local resection of ampullary tumors, because of high recurrence rate and lack of established management criteria. Recently, endoscopic papillectomy (ESP) has been accepted as an alternative therapy to surgery in ampullary adenoma. According to some studies, the tumor size criterion for endoscopic papillectomy is up to 4–4.5 cm.5–7 Nevertheless, ampullary adenoma is considered as a precancerous lesion, and the rate of development of carcinoma has been shown to be 30%.8 Moreover, the coexistence of carcinoma within adenoma cannot be excluded by preprocedural biopsy.9,10

As described above, the management strategies for ampulla adenoma are expanding; nevertheless, the criteria for its surgical management have not yet been established. Therefore, we undertook this study to analyze the data of presumptive ampullary adenoma, including three procedures (ESP, TDR, and PD).

Patients and Methods

Between July 1997 and July 2008, a total of 33 patients who were diagnosed with ampullary adenoma underwent endoscopic or surgical management at Ajou University Medical Center, Suwon, Korea. Management procedures performed were 20 endoscopic papillectomies, 5 transduodenal resections, and 8 pancreatoduodenectomies. Twenty-three patients were male and ten patients were female, whose age ranged from 26 to 80 years. We analyzed the data according to both preprocedural and final pathology. After excluding 4 patients who did not undergo preprocedural biopsy, 29 patients were enrolled in the analysis of preprocedural pathology. The tumor size was established from review of final pathology. The patients who were diagnosed with cancer by initial pathology and who underwent palliative procedures were excluded.

We collected consecutive identification of patients who underwent surgical management after endoscopic papillectomy. Moreover, we were able to identify patients who underwent ESP from the endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) database at the Department of Gastroenterology. We retrospectively reviewed medical and surgical records of each patient and analyzed characteristics of patients, findings during ERCP, procedure-related complications, imaging findings, surgical procedures, and postoperative outcome. All pathologic reports were re-reviewed by one pathologist. Pathologically, the ampullary tumors were classified as low-grade dysplasia (LGD), high-grade dysplasia (HGD) or carcinoma, according to the Vienna classification of gastrointestinal epithelial neoplasia.11

Surgical procedures were undertaken by two experienced hepatobiliary surgeons. During the study period, 80 PDs for benign or malignant ampullary tumors were carried out in our hospital. We retrospectively reviewed the results of pathology and selected eight patients who were preoperatively diagnosed with ampullary adenoma. Initial PDs were indicated in patients with evidence of intraductal growth or large tumor size, and in patients who were willing to undergo surgery. All patients underwent pylorus-preserving pancreatoduodenectomy.

TDR was indicated in patients with high operative risk of PD. Moreover, we examined the gross nature of mass intraoperatively and then carried out operation. If we realized that the tumor was fixed to the duodenum, TDR was converted to PD. The operative procedure was started after longitudinal duodenotomy along the second portion of the duodenum. A full-thickness excision of the ampulla was undertaken, including adequate margin around papilla. Sphincteroplasty (common bile duct and pancreatic duct) was carried out using interrupted 5-0 Prolene sutures. The duodenotomy was closed longitudinally, most commonly in two layers of interrupted sutures. Lymph node dissection was not performed in any patients.

ESP was indicated in patients who had no evidence of intraductal growth into the bile duct and/or pancreatic duct on ERCP or endoscopic ultrasonography (EUS), regardless of the result of preprocedural biopsy. Furthermore, ESP was also performed when patients did not want to undergo surgery despite young age. Written informed consent was obtained from all patients. All procedures were carried out by two experienced endoscopists.

Imaging studies for both diagnosis and recurrence of ampullary tumors included endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP), abdominal computed tomography (CT), and endoscopic ultrasonography (EUS). All patients did not undergo routine preprocedural endoscopic biopsy and endoscopic ultrasonography.

Statistical analysis was performed with chi-square test, Fisher’s exact test, Mann–Whitney U test, and Spearman’s correlation. The disease-free survival rates were calculated using the Kaplan–Meier method and using the log-rank test. P-value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

ESP: The final pathology of 20 patients was LGD in 13 (65.0%), HGD in 5 (25.0%), and cancer in 2 (10.0%). Tumor involvement of lateral margin was found in 8 of 20 patients (40.0%), and only 2 of 8 patients showed tumor recurrence. Procedure-related mortality rate was 5.0% (n = 1; necrotizing pancreatitis). The mean follow-up period was 20.0 ± 18.0 months. Of 20 patients, 6 patients (30.0%) experienced recurrence after endoscopic papillectomy: 4 patients presented with cancers whereas 2 patients presented with LGD. Two patients finally underwent surgical management because of recurrence of ampullary tumors.

TDR: The comorbidities of patients included brain tumor (n = 1), hypertension (n = 2), and brain infarction (n = 1). There was no lateral margin involvement or recurrence. One patient underwent reoperation due to postoperative bleeding. On reoperation, active bleeding was found, and suture ligation was done. However, rebleeding occurred on the fifth day after reoperation. Gastroduodenal and inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries were embolized by angiography. Finally, the patient died of multiple organ failure.

PD: Seven patients were male and one patient was female, whose mean age was 54.7 ± 10.9 years. The results of preoperative biopsy showed three LGD and five HGD. However, final pathology revealed adenocarcinoma in seven patients. According to pathologic reports, nodal involvement was present in two patients, and lymphovascular invasion and perineural invasion were found in one patient each. The mean follow-up period was 60.6 ± 43.5 months. The median disease-free survival was 84.0 months; systemic or local recurrence appeared in four patients.

Discussion

For the surgical management of ampullary tumors, pancreatoduodenectomy has widely been accepted by surgeons.1–3 In addition, recent reports indicate that PD is an acceptable procedure to manage ampullary tumor because of low mortality rate, although morbidity rate is high.12,13 Therefore, it stands to reason that ampullary cancer is managed surgically.

Nevertheless, inability to preoperatively detect ampullary cancer with endoscopic biopsies and imaging study still poses a serious problem.10,14–16 Ampullary adenoma is well known as a premalignant lesion because of adenoma–carcinoma sequence, similar to colonic cancer.17,18 However, the coexistence of carcinoma within the adenoma cannot be excluded by preprocedural biopsy.9,10 Moreover, the false-negative rate of endoscopic biopsy for cancer has been reported to be high, ranging from 11.7% to 60%.3,9,19 In the present study, the false-negative rate of biopsy was 27.5%, similar to in other reports. Seifert et al. reported that endoscopic forceps biopsy of ampullary tumors does not rule out the possibility of deeper cancer.8 Therefore, some authors advocate deeper biopsy after sphincterotomy for accurate diagnosis of endoscopic biopsy.20–22 On prospective study, however, Menzel et al. reported that sensitivity was found to be 21% before and 37% after sphincterotomy, and concluded that endoscopic forceps biopsies do not allow for reliable preoperative diagnosis of ampullary tumors.15 It is important to point out that management strategies can be determined by the result of endoscopic biopsy. Indeed, when biopsy makes a mistake, the result could be detrimental to patient. Therefore, the more accurate method of endoscopic biopsy and safe management strategy are needed.

There is no clear consensus for the management of pre-HGD, because some authors fear both high coexistence of cancer and high recurrence. Meneghetti et al. and Heidecke et al. reported the rate of coexistence of cancer in patients with pre-HGD as 100% and 44%, respectively.23,24 For recurrence of pre-HGD in 12 patients with benign lesions, 3 patients with moderate- or high-grade dysplasia encountered recurrence.25 On the other hand, some authors reported no recurrence in patients with pre-HGD, and one study including pre-HGD did not describe detailed outcomes.3,26,27 In the present study, five of ten patients with pre-HGD were identified as cancer on final pathology. Moreover, we found that patients with pre-HGD presented more recurrence than did patients with pre-LGD.

In general, the presence of HGD on preoperative biopsy would lead surgeons to decide on a pancreaticoduodenectomy.3 Moreover, as a gastroenterologist, Katsinelos et al. referred three patients with pre-HGD to the department of surgery in their study.28 On the other hand, Yoon et al. and Bohnacker et al. reported that endoscopic papillectomy may be a curative treatment in patients with HGD.29,30 In the present study, preprocedural HGD was associated highly with both coexistence of cancer and recurrence. Therefore, we suggest that patients with preprocedural HGD should undergo pancreatoduodenectomy, if they do not have high operative risk.

According to many reports, criteria for surgical ampullectomy include age, tumor stage, tumor size, nodal involvement, and combined illness.31,32 Rattner et al. reported tumor size (<3 cm) as criterion for ampullectomy; however, this study was composed entirely of two surgical managements (14 ampullectomy versus 12 pancreatoduodenectomy).31 On the other hand, criteria of endoscopic papillectomy included size (<4–4.5 cm), no evidence of intraductal growth, and no evidence of malignancy by endoscopic findings (ulceration, friability, and spontaneous bleeding).5–7 However, none of the above studies was analyzed by means of tumor size. Furthermore, the tumor size was determined at their discretion with no evidence. In our present study, initial endoscopic gross features of ampullary tumors appeared to be benign, neither friability nor ulceration.

In fact, the size criteria of management for ampullary adenoma have not yet been established at either departments of surgery and gastroenterology. In our present study, the results of analysis of tumor size showed that large (>1.5 cm) tumor may be considered as HGD/cancer, and tends to be treated by surgery rather than endoscopic management. Although our size criterion is too small, nevertheless, it can be a safe recommendation to surgeons for management of presumptive ampullary adenoma. In addition, it is a strong point of the present study that analysis of ampullary tumors included three procedures (ESP, TDR, and PD). On the other hand, Maneghetti et al. reported that tumor size did not influence recurrence rate and was not a predictor of the coexistence of malignancy, while other authors reported that tumors up to 7 cm in diameter were successfully resected by endoscopy.23,33,34

Some authors have recommended transduodenal resection with intraoperative frozen section for small benign lesions, because the morbidity and mortality of PD are high.9,31,35 Moreover, Beger et al. and Grobmyer et al. reported no recurrence after TDR for ampullary adenoma with HGD.3,26 Although one study advocated the presence of recurrence after TDR, it may be acceptable for ampullary adenoma with HGD.25 However, the results of intraoperative frozen biopsy are variable, and therefore may not be reliable. Grobmyer et al. and Meneghetti et al. reported that frozen-section biopsy was less reliable than expected.23,26 On the other hand, Clary et al. advocated that frozen-section evaluation of ampullary neoplasms was accurate.9 In the present study, we did not use the intraoperative frozen biopsy because of no gross suspicion of cancer. Roggin et al. reported that conversion to PD should be considered when intraoperative or final pathology identifies adenocarcinoma after transduodenal ampullectomy.36 If intraoperative frozen biopsy did not reveal cancer whereas postoperative result was cancer, the patient should undergo relaparotomy. Therefore, the endoscopic papillectomy may be useful not only for more accurate diagnosis prior to surgery, but also for curative therapy of ampullary tumors. Some authors undertook endoscopic papillectomy without preprocedural biopsy and then decided the modality of treatment next.37

In this study, the characteristics of patients with preprocedural HGD included high coexistence of cancer and high recurrence rate, and large tumor was associated with both surgical management and ampullary cancer. Moreover, ampullary tumors of more than 1.5 cm in size increase the possibility of HGD or cancer by approximately three times. Therefore, we recommend PD or TDR in patients diagnosed with ampullary adenoma with preprocedural HGD or more than 1.5 cm in size. In addition, endoscopic resection can be recommended as a diagnostic tool prior to surgery, because of high false-negative rate of biopsy. These recommendations are expected to improve diagnostic accuracy of presumptive ampullary adenoma.
